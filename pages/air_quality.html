Air Quality

Aim

The aim of this project is to analyze the spatial and temporal patterns of air pollution — specifically NO2, PM2.5, and PM10 — over a selected case study area using CAMS reanalysis data. The analysis is based on monthly and yearly aggregated datasets for the year 2022. Data in NetCDF format are processed and visualized through QGIS by converting them into raster layers using the native mesh layer functionalities. The project culminates in the creation of average concentration maps for each pollutant, which enable the evaluation of air quality trends and the identification of critical areas in relation to current EU air quality standards.
We will use land the multi-temporal air quality data (Jan 2013 to Dec 2022) from CAMS Reanalysis Data .

Processing

Processing and Visualization of CAMS Air Quality Data Using NetCDF and QGIS

The NetCDF (Network Common Data Form) format is a widely adopted standard in atmospheric and environmental sciences for storing multidimensional scientific datasets, such as time series of pollutant concentrations across spatial grids. It is particularly suitable for handling data from services like CAMS (Copernicus Atmosphere Monitoring Service) due to its efficiency in managing large-scale model outputs and its support for self-describing metadata (e.g., units, variable descriptions), which makes it compatible with various analysis tools including Python (xarray), Panoply, and QGIS.

In the context of the project, CAMS NetCDF files were imported and processed in QGIS as mesh layers, which allow for the visualization and manipulation of time-varying geospatial data. After assigning the EPSG:4326 coordinate reference system, the dataset was explored and processed using tools such as the Mesh Calculator to compute monthly average concentrations of selected pollutants. These aggregated datasets were subsequently exported as GeoTIFF raster files, resulting in a series of average concentration maps. This approach enabled a clear and accessible spatial representation of air pollution levels, serving as a key component of the project’s analytical framework.

Conversion of land cover to vectors and for each computation of zonal statistics

For each pollutant we wanted to compute the minimum, maximum and mean value of the concentration of the pollutant over some specific land cover classes. We were particularly interested in comparing Settlement, Agriculture, Grassland and Forest. For doing so, we converted the land cover raster data to vector data and then we computed the zonal statistics for each land cover class. We repeated this for each year from 2013 to 2022, but for simplicity for all of these we used the classification map of 2022.
